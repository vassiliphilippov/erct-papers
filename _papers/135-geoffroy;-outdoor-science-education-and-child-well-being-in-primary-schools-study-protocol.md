paper_link: https://doi.org/10.3389/feduc.2026.1791684
abstract: >-
  Background: Children are increasingly exposed to stressors related to
  urbanization, climate instability, and biodiversity loss, which may contribute
  to poor well-being. School-based outdoor education has shown potential to
  support both well-being and learning, but robust evidence from randomized
  trials remains scarce. Science education outdoors, by engaging students with
  local biodiversity, may enhance scientific observation skills while
  contributing to public health goals such as stress reduction and connection to
  nature.

  Methods: This cluster randomized controlled trial (cRCT) will compare outdoor
  versus classroom-based science instruction among 5th- and 6th-grade students
  in primary schools in the greater Montreal area (Québec, Canada). Forty
  classes (≈1,000 students) will be recruited across two waves (2025–2026).
  Teachers will be randomized (1:1) to teach life science content either
  outdoors (intervention) or indoors (comparator) for 2 h per week over 5 weeks.
  The primary outcome is students’ scientific observation skills, assessed
  through structured interviews and a validated observation framework. Secondary
  outcomes include eco-anxiety, perceived stress, connection to nature, and
  basic psychological needs satisfaction measured with standardized
  questionnaires. Analyses will use mixed-effects regression models to account
  for clustering at the class and school levels.

  Discussion: This study will implement a cluster randomized controlled design
  to examine the effects of outdoor education on students’ science learning,
  stress, eco-anxiety, and psychosocial factors. By embedding the intervention
  in the regular science curriculum, the trial aims to generate ecologically
  valid evidence relevant to both education and public health sectors. Despite
  the relatively short intervention duration, the study is designed to inform
  intersectoral initiatives addressing health-promoting school environments and
  to generate evidence regarding the role of nature-based learning in urban
  settings.
