title: >-

abstract: >-
  We conducted a parallel-group randomized controlled trial in
  routine calligraphy classes, comparing smartphone-based
  augmented reality with conventional model copying in twenty
  adults. The instructor, copybook, venue, and contact time were
  held constant. The primary outcome—calligraphy self-efficacy—
  improved more in the augmented reality group at post-test and
  at the one-week follow-up. Secondary outcomes showed a
  consistent but non-significant advantage for momentary flow.
  Process efficiency also improved, with fewer revision cycles
  per character, shorter time to criterion, and a higher
  completion rate, while subjective workload was similar between
  groups. Blinded expert ratings favored augmented reality for
  stroke-order fidelity, stroke continuity, component
  proportion, and overall balance. These findings support the
  feasibility of aligning process visualization with the
  temporal and structural demands of calligraphy. They also
  warrant larger and longer trials with multimethod assessment.
